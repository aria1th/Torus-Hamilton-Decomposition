# D5 Researcher 2 — symmetry / asymmetry after the 072 bridge sync

## Scope

This note updates the `071` symmetry question after the `072` context sync.
The point is to answer the fork

> is the chain offset globally removable, or is carry essentially asymmetric
> across chains?

with the **decorated exact bridge** as the target object, not the bare
`m`-state bridge.

Inputs actually used here:

- `068B` exact-reduction support for the global `beta` clock and marked-chain
  structure;
- `070` corner-time descent theorem, which says that once a deterministic
  quotient is exact for current `epsilon4`, the short-corner detector and the
  canonical clock descend automatically;
- `071` marked-chain notes, especially the affine local gauge
  `s = q + sigma` and the working decorated beta-chain picture;
- a direct `073` recheck on the frozen `047` dataset, saved separately as
  `d5_researcher2_decorated_symmetry_support_073.json`.

What I do **not** claim in this note:

- I do not prove that `(\beta,a,b)` is the *smallest* exact bridge.
- I do not prove that the decoration bits already descend to the intended local
  admissible class.
- I do not extend the direct decorated-event check past the frozen checked range
  `m = 5,7,9,11`.

So the theorem-strength here is:

- a clean **symmetry / asymmetry theorem on the checked range** for the refined
  bridge question;
- larger-modulus support only for the underlying global `beta` normalization,
  not for the full decorated exact bridge.

## Executive answer

The updated answer is now sharp:

1. the chain offset **is** globally removable at the bridge level;
2. carry is therefore **rotatable**;
3. the obstruction to the bare bridge is **not** an intrinsic carry asymmetry;
4. the real surviving asymmetry is a finite **chain decoration**, visible in the
   two early post-carry sites.

So the correct picture is neither

- “no bridge exists,” nor
- “the bare `m`-state clock is already exact,”

but rather:

> the global clock coordinate exists, but exactness for current `epsilon4`
> requires a decorated bridge.

Equivalently: **offset asymmetry is removable; decoration asymmetry is what
remains.**

## 1. What changes from the earlier `071` obstruction

The earlier `071` local obstruction was real, but it targeted the wrong object.
It showed that the per-chain affine gauge

\[
s = q + \sigma,\qquad \sigma = source_u + w \pmod m
\]

does not globalize by itself, and that `s` (even with the endpoint mark) does
not define the exact global quotient on the union of chains.

That remains correct.

But after the `072` sync, the bridge problem is no longer:

> can the exact global bridge be recovered directly from the unnormalized local
> gauge?

The coarse bridge is now treated as settled: the per-chain marked-chain
quotients already glue at the carry / distance-to-endpoint level.
So the real symmetry question has shifted to:

> once the global clock is normalized, is there still an essential carry
> asymmetry, or only a small event decoration?

That is the question answered below.

## 2. Global normalized clock

On the theorem-side active branch, define

\[
\beta := -(q+s+v+\lambda) \pmod m.
\]

The `068B` support already says that on the exhaustive regenerated full-row
range through `m = 21`:

- `beta` has exact unit drift,
- the safer first exact object is the marked length-`m` chain,
- and `(B,beta)` is exact for `q`, `c`, `epsilon4`, `tau`, `next_tau`, and
  `next_B`.

So the clock normalization itself is no longer the live issue.
The live issue is only what extra chain data must be kept together with `beta`
for **current-event exactness**.

## 3. Decorated beta-chain theorem on the checked range

Take a regular state-level marked chain and let `beta = m-1` at its
`carry_jump` start.
The checked `071` working picture says that, on `m = 5,7,9,11`, every such
chain has one of four event patterns, indexed by `(a,b) in {0,1}^2`:

- `(0,0)`:
  `carry_jump, flat, flat, ..., flat, wrap`
- `(0,1)`:
  `carry_jump, flat, other_0010, flat, ..., flat, wrap`
- `(1,0)`:
  `carry_jump, other_1000, flat, ..., flat, wrap`
- `(1,1)`:
  `carry_jump, other_1000, other_0010, flat, ..., flat, wrap`

where

- `a` records whether the `beta = m-2` state is `other_1000`,
- `b` records whether the `beta = m-3` state is `other_0010`.

This gives the natural candidate quotient

\[
\Pi(x) = (\beta(x), a(C_x), b(C_x)),
\]

where `C_x` is the unique regular marked chain containing `x`.
The successor rule is simply

\[
(\beta,a,b) \mapsto (\beta-1,a,b),
\]

and the current-event scheduler is

\[
\epsilon_4(\beta,a,b)=
\begin{cases}
\mathrm{carry\_jump}, & \beta=m-1,\\
\mathrm{other}_{1000}, & \beta=m-2\ \&\ a=1,\\
\mathrm{other}_{0010}, & \beta=m-3\ \&\ b=1,\\
\mathrm{wrap}, & \beta=0,\\
\mathrm{flat}, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\]

So the chain-dependent offset has disappeared from the **transport law**.
What remains is only the fixed chain decoration `(a,b)`.

## 4. Bare-bridge obstruction

The bare `m`-state bridge keeps only `beta` and forgets `(a,b)`.
That cannot be exact for current `epsilon4` as soon as different chain types
occur.

### Proposition

On the checked range `m = 5,7,9,11`, no quotient that factors only through
`beta` can be exact for current `epsilon4` on the regular marked chains.

### Proof

Take two regular chains with different `a` values.
At `beta = m-2`, both chains have the same `beta`, but one current event is
`other_1000` and the other is `flat`.
So `epsilon4` does not factor through `beta`.

Likewise, take two regular chains with different `b` values.
At `beta = m-3`, both chains have the same `beta`, but one current event is
`other_0010` and the other is `flat`.
So again `epsilon4` does not factor through `beta`.

The `073` recheck above shows that all tested moduli have multiple chain types,
so this obstruction is realized on every checked modulus. ∎

This is the clean counterexample-style obstruction that `Researcher 2` wanted.
It rules out the **bare** rotational picture, but it does **not** rule out the
refined decorated one.

## 5. Symmetry versus asymmetry: the correct split

We can now state the symmetry answer cleanly.

### Theorem (checked-range symmetry / asymmetry split)

On the regular marked-chain union in the checked frozen range:

1. the chain offset is globally removable at the level of the normalized clock
   `beta`;
2. carry is rotatable;
3. exactness for current `epsilon4` nevertheless requires finite chain
   decoration;
4. the bare `m`-state bridge is too coarse, but a decorated bridge of the form
   `(\beta,a,b)` is supported.

So the surviving asymmetry across chains is **not** a carry asymmetry and **not**
an unremovable phase offset. It is a small decoration attached to the global
clock.

## 6. Consequence for the realization route

Combined with the `070` corner-time descent theorem, this sharpens the live D5
frontier further.

Once the right deterministic decorated quotient is exact for current
`epsilon4`, the rest is automatic:

- the short-corner detector descends,
- corner-time is well-defined,
- the canonical clock descends by corner-time.

So the live question is no longer

> does any bridge exist,

and no longer

> is the clock coordinate global,

but only

> what is the smallest exact decorated bridge for current `epsilon4`, and does
> that decoration descend in the intended admissible class?

## 7. Bottom line for Researcher 2

The answer to the original symmetry fork should now be recorded as:

> **The offset is globally removable, so carry is rotatable.**
> **What is genuinely asymmetric across chains is only a small post-carry
> decoration, not the clock itself.**

In particular:

- the old “essential asymmetry” picture is too strong;
- the old “bare rotatable clock” picture is too weak;
- the right replacement is a **decorated rotatable clock**.

That is the precise theorem / obstruction split distinguishing the two pictures
at the `072` frontier.
