The remaining issue looks primarily expository rather than mathematical. The global structure of the argument has improved substantially, but that gain creates a local presentation cost if the reader is asked to hold three parallel constructions in mind at once. The cleanest resolution is to package them as a single logical progression rather than as three coequal objects.

## 1. Present the proof as one narrative, not three parallel variants

The main packaging change should be to avoid presenting the Primary Geometry, the deleted variant, and the final Route E construction as separate versions of the same idea. That framing increases reader load without adding mathematical value.

Instead, the proof can be organized as:

1. a natural base geometry;
2. a precise obstruction in that base geometry;
3. a short no-go argument showing that a local repair cannot work;
4. the forced bulk surgery that resolves the obstruction.

This gives the reader a single evolving story rather than a comparison of multiple constructions.

## 2. Promote the primary geometry and demote the deleted variant

The Primary Geometry should be introduced as the natural baseline model, not as a failed preliminary attempt. It already explains the even-case behavior for Colors 0 and 2, so it deserves to be treated as the structural starting point of the argument.

By contrast, the deleted variant does not need to appear as a full construction. Its real purpose is narrower: it answers the natural question of whether one can preserve the $+3$ interior flow and repair the problem only by adjusting boundary points.

That purpose is better served by a short no-go proposition based on the explicit noninjectivity witnesses. The reader then learns the important fact, namely that boundary-only patching fails, without having to track a third formal object through the paper.

## 3. Reframe the Case II family as a forced topological repair

Once the no-go statement is in place, the added Case II affine family no longer reads as an ad hoc modification. It becomes the mechanism required to alter the interior dynamics themselves.

This is the most useful framing:

- the base geometry leaves Color 1 trapped in the residue-3 obstruction when $m \equiv 4 \pmod 6$;
- local boundary corrections do not remove that obstruction;
- therefore a bulk transversal defect is required to change the effective shift from $+3$ to $+2/+6$.

Presented this way, the construction reads as a forced topological repair rather than an engineering patch.

## 4. Use the mod-12 split for Color 0 as a structural payoff

The Color 0 mod-12 split is worth emphasizing because it shows that the new framework is explanatory, not merely corrective.

The key point is that the split does not arise from a separate mechanism. It is a cyclic reordering within the same residue-4 splice picture, driven by the intercept term in the relevant affine height. That is exactly the kind of secondary payoff that reassures the reader that the geometry has been properly understood.

## 5. Suggested manuscript packaging

A clean rewrite would follow this order:

1. Introduce the primary geometry as the uniform base model.
2. State the Color 1 obstruction at $m \equiv 4 \pmod 6$.
3. Insert the short no-go proposition showing that boundary-only corrections destroy injectivity.
4. Introduce the bounded height-comparison machinery and derive the Case II bulk repair.
5. Close with the finite splice and the Color 0 mod-12 interpretation as part of the same geometric framework.

The central expository goal is simple: replace the feeling of "three related constructions" with a single chain of necessity, from base model to obstruction to forced repair. That seems to be the most readable form of the argument now available.
