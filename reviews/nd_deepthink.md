Generalizing the current lane-time cylinder model to an $n$-dimensional discrete torus $\mathbb Z_m^n$ is a natural next question, but it should not be viewed as a routine extension of the present proof. The proof mechanisms that are effective in the one-dimensional transverse setting depend heavily on low-dimensional order structure, and several of them change qualitatively in higher dimension.

What follows is the cleanest way to state the main barriers.

## 1. Geometric barrier: from interval structure to hyperplane arrangements

The current argument relies on a one-dimensional transverse parameter. Defect functions are affine lines, and two such lines intersect only at isolated points. Those critical points divide the domain into intervals on which the defect order, and hence the itinerary, is constant.

In $\mathbb Z_m^n$, the analogous defects are affine hyperplanes. Their intersections no longer partition the domain into intervals, but into a hyperplane arrangement with many polytopal cells. The corresponding first-return dynamics is therefore much closer to a polyhedral exchange system than to the interval-exchange-type structure used in the present proof. That makes it much harder to extract clean arithmetic families or stable itinerary formulas.

## 2. Algebraic barrier: from scalar congruences to lattice invariants

In the current setting, the key residue phenomena are controlled by a small number of scalar congruence conditions, such as mod $3$, mod $4$, or mod $6$.

In higher dimension, locating orbit intersections with affine defects requires solving systems of linear congruences in several variables. The relevant denominators and compatibility conditions are then governed by determinants, sublattice indices, and Smith normal form data rather than by a single modulus. So the arithmetic structure becomes a lattice-theoretic problem, not just a residue-class calculation.

## 3. Topological barrier: from a single transversal repair to multi-directional homology

In the present argument, the obstruction can be repaired by introducing a single macroscopic transversal defect that changes the effective strand dynamics in a controlled way.

On an $n$-dimensional torus, the cycle structure can interact with several independent homology directions at once. A higher-dimensional repair surface may fix one obstruction while simultaneously introducing new collisions or unwanted interactions in other directions. In other words, the splice mechanism is no longer a one-parameter braiding problem; it becomes a genuinely multi-directional topological design problem.

## 4. Combinatorial barrier: the finite splice principle no longer has fixed size

The present finite splice argument works because the exceptional boundary behavior is concentrated in a bounded and explicitly manageable set of lanes.

In higher dimension, the natural analogue of the boundary is an $(n-1)$-dimensional face, so the number of sites that may need explicit correction grows on the order of $m^{n-1}$. Unless a new compression principle is found, the splice graph is no longer of bounded size. That removes one of the main reasons the current proof closes cleanly.

## 5. Recommended positioning in the paper

The right conclusion is not that higher-dimensional generalization is impossible. It is that such a generalization would require new ideas and should be framed as future work rather than as an omitted corollary.

A concise discussion paragraph could make the following points:

- the one-dimensional transverse model is tractable because affine defects induce a controlled interval-like ordering;
- higher-dimensional generalization would replace that structure by hyperplane arrangements, multi-variable congruence constraints, and more complicated splice topology;
- the present paper should therefore emphasize the low-dimensional model as the regime in which the geometric surgery remains rigid enough to analyze completely.

That is a stronger and cleaner message than promising an immediate extension. It positions the current result as a fully controlled low-dimensional theory and identifies the higher-dimensional case as a distinct research program.
